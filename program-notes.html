Program Notes

Portrait in Seven Shades is a seven-movement suite by composer and saxophonist Ted Nash of the Jazz at Lincoln Center Orchestra. Rather than describing famous paintings with words, Nash uses music to explore the style, personality, and creative world of seven major artists: Monet, Dalí, Matisse, Picasso, Van Gogh, Chagall, and Pollock.

Nash chose painters who lived and worked during roughly the same 100-year period in which jazz itself developed. Both art forms went through major changes during that time, and Nash highlights the parallels — how both painters and musicians think about color, form, structure, and expression.

As a longtime member of the Jazz at Lincoln Center Orchestra, Nash wrote this music with specific players in mind, shaping each movement around the orchestra's strengths. The suite also calls for guest musicians on violin, accordion, and tuba, as well as extensive woodwind doubling, giving each movement a unique sonic palette.

Below is a brief overview of the seven movements:

1. Monet

Inspired by the soft colors and blurred edges of Monet's Water-Lily paintings. Nash uses warm harmonies, floating woodwind textures, and gentle rhythms to capture that sense of atmosphere and light.

Click Here to View Monet's Waterlilies (MoMA)

2. Dalí

Dalí's surreal, dream-like images — including The Persistence of Memory — guide this movement. Odd time signatures, unexpected harmonies, and a blues foundation create a musical world that feels familiar and strange at the same time.

Click Here to View Dalí's The Persistence of Memory (MoMA)

3. Matisse

Playful rhythms and bright colors drive this movement, reflecting Matisse's bold use of shape and simplicity. Influences from Thelonious Monk can be felt in the quirkiness and rhythmic bounce.

Click Here to View Matisse's Danse (MoMA)

4. Picasso

Picasso's multiple artistic phases — from romantic portraits to cubism — are reflected in shifting musical sections. Nash uses intervals of fourths and layered lines to mirror Picasso's fragmented, geometric style.

Click Here to View Picasso's Girl Before A Mirror (MoMA)

5. Van Gogh

This movement is the most song-like and emotional. It references The Starry Night and other works, reflecting Van Gogh's intensity, vulnerability, and expressive brushwork. Nash even wrote lyrics for part of this movement.

Click Here to View Van Gogh's Starry Night (MoMA)

6. Chagall

Chagall's art often features floating figures, village scenes, and strong ties to Eastern European culture. Accordion, violin, and klezmer-influenced rhythms help create that sense of storytelling and community.

Click Here to View Chagall's I and the Village (MoMA)

7. Pollock

Using the idea of paint splatters and abstract motion, this movement features short musical fragments tossed between players, gradually forming a larger groove. Improvisation plays a big role here, reflecting the energy of Pollock's technique.

Click Here to View Pollock's White Light (MoMA)
